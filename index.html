2.1   INTRODUCTION

You are familiar with the concepts of work and energy in the context of mechanics. In this chapter, we will see how these concepts can be applied to electric forces and electric fields. In a gravitational field (like that of the earth), a material body experiences a gravitational force and the field does work during its displacement. This work is expressed in terms of gravitational potential energy. Likewise, when a charged particle moves in an electric field, the field exerts a force that can do work on the particle. This work can be expressed in terms of electrical potential energy.

 

The concept of potential energy is central to the understanding of a conservative field (such as gravitational and electric fields). In this chapter, we will discuss work, potential energy and conservation of energy in electrostatic fields and see how useful these are in understanding and analysing electrical interactions.

2.12   ELECTROSTATICS OF CONDUCTORS

We have already discussed about metals or conducting materials in previous chapter also. Here we shall quickly recapitulate previous knowledge and add some new points to it. Main difference between metallic conductors and insulators is that metallic conductors have electrons as free charge. In an atom electrons orbit around the nucleus and it is very difficult for them to leave their orbits, but in case of metals some of the electrons belonging to outer orbits of several atoms become free. These free electrons exist like connected cloud kind of thing inside a conductor that readily responds to external electric field.

Due to the presence of free electrons inside conductors they are electrically more sensitive than insulators or dielectrics. How do dielectrics respond to external electric field? That we shall study in a later section. For now let us consider only conductors.

(i) In electrostatic condition the net electric field intensity inside conductor becomes zero: As explained in the previous chapter, free electrons of the metal or conductor move opposite to applied electric field, and thus charge imbalance created due to this, creates an electric field opposite to the applied field.

This electric field increases as more and more electrons are moved. But after some time this field becomes equal to applied field and net field becomes zero. Since there is no electric field hence no electric lines will exist inside the conductors. Migration of electrons stops only when electric field intensity becomes zero everywhere inside the conductor. In other words, we can say that electric lines of forces always originate or terminate at the surface of the metal in electrostatic condition. They cannot exist inside the volume of the metallic conductor in electrostatic condition.

(ii) In electrostatic condition the net charge always come to the outer surface of a conductor: We can easily explain this using Gaussâ€™s law. Electric field intensity inside a conductor is zero everywhere, in electrostatic condition. If we visualise any kind of Gaussian surface inside the conductor, then due to zero field, electric flux must remain zero, hence no net charge must be enclosed by this Gaussian surface. It will be true even if we select almost the entire volume of the conductor by selecting Gaussian surface just below the outer surface of the conductor.

(iii) In electrostatic condition the electric field lines are always normal to the surface of a conductor: If we assume that electric field lines or say, electric field intensity is not perpendicular to a metallic or conducting surface then there will be a component of electric field intensity along the surface. Free electrons of that metallic or conducting surface will move due to this component of field that is parallel to the surface. It will be equivalent of saying that electrostatic condition has not yet been attained. As soon as the condition of electrostatics is attained, electric field intensity becomes perpendicular to the surface of the conductor.

(iv) In electrostatic condition the whole volume of a conductor is equipotential: If we look at the following relation then we know that potential difference between any two points in some region of space is zero, if electric field intensity in that region is zero. Hence, the potential remains constant in a zero electric field region. Inside the conductor there is no electric field, thus the value of electric potential must be same throughout its volume. Electric field intensity just below the metal surface is zero.

Therefore, the potential must not change if we move from the surface towards the volume of the conductor. Hence, electric potential inside the volume of the conductor must be same as that on its surface. In point (iii), we have learned that electric lines are perpendicular to surface of the conductor. Hence, the outer surface of conductor can also be treated as an equipotential surface.

(v) The surfaces of two charged metal plates, parallel to each other always carry equal and opposite charges: Electric field near large metal plates is always perpendicular to their surface. In case of finite sized plates, the same condition is valid if a point lies very near to the surface. In Fig. 4.1. two charged metallic plates are placed parallel to each other. Let us select a Gaussian surface in the form of a cuboid in such a manner that two faces of metal plates facing each other lie inside the Gaussian surface.

Four faces of a cuboidal Gaussian surface are perpendicular to metal plates and hence parallel to possible electric field in between the plates. Hence, the angle between area vector and electric field intensity is 90o for these four faces. Electric flux associated with these four faces of cuboid becomes zero, as it is the dot product of area vector and electric field intensity.

Now for two faces, which lie inside metal plates, the flux associated with them is zero because they lie in an electric field free region.

Electrostatic field intensity inside metal is zero.

The final conclusion is that total electric flux associated with a cuboidal Gaussian surface selected above is zero. According to Gaussâ€™s theorem, electric flux for three-dimensional closed surfaces is . Hence, enclosed charge within a Gaussian surface must be zero, to explain zero flux as explained above.

Net charge can be zero only in either of the cases: first, when no charge is carried by any surface or second, when the surfaces carry equal and opposite charges to make net zero. That is why, charged metal objects that face each other always induce equal and opposite charges on each other.

A Gaussian surface in the form of cuboid is selected in such a manner that one of the faces of the cuboid parallel to the charged conducting surface remains inside the metal object. Electric flux is zero for this surface because electric field inside the conductor is zero. Four faces of cuboid are perpendicular to metal surface.

Electric flux for these four is zero because the angle between electric field and surface normal is 90Â°. Flux is associated with only one face of the cube. 2.13   DIELECTRICS AND POLARIZATION

In case of dielectric or insulating materials, free electrons are not available like in the case of metals. We have already discussed about dielectric materials in the previous chapter. So this discussion must be taken as a continuation. We earlier learned that there are two possible types of molecules of dielectric materials. One is polar and the other is non-polar. When the dielectric materials are placed inside an external electric field, then the non-polar molecules are stretched and polar molecules get aligned in the direction of external electric field. In both cases, the result is that the material develops net electric dipole moment.
